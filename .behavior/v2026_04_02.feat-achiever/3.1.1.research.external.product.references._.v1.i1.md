# research: references — achiever role

## purpose

this document consolidates authoritative references that ground the achiever role design. these sources provide academic foundation, industry standards, and practitioner wisdom.

---

## foundational academic sources

### [1] locke & latham — goal theory

**source:** locke, e. a., & latham, g. p. (2002). "build a practically useful theory of goal set and task motivation." american psychologist, 57(9), 705-717.

**type:** [academic] — 35-year research program, 400+ studies

**key quote:** "specific, difficult goals lead to higher performance than do easy goals, 'do your best' goals, or no goals."

**relevance:** establishes that goals with specific outcomes outperform vague intentions. validates achiever's requirement for `what.outcome` and `how.gate`.

---

### [2] locke & latham — goal mechanisms

**source:** locke, e. a., & latham, g. p. (2006). "new directions in goal theory." current directions in psychological science, 15(5), 265-268.

**type:** [academic]

**key quote:** "goals affect performance through four mechanisms: direction, effort, persistence, and task strategies."

**relevance:** explains WHY goals work. achiever's schema captures direction (`what.outcome`), and task strategies (`how.task`).

---

### [3] newell & simon — problem space

**source:** newell, a., & simon, h. a. (1972). "human problem solve." prentice-hall.

**type:** [book] — foundational cognitive science

**key quote:** "problem space consists of a goal state and the operators that can be applied to reach it."

**relevance:** distinguishes goal (desired state) from task (operator). achiever separates `what.outcome` from `how.task` based on this distinction.

---

### [4] oettingen — mental contrast

**source:** oettingen, g. (2012). "future thought and behaviour change." european review of social psychology, 23(1), 1-63.

**type:** [academic]

**key quote:** "wishes without implementation plans rarely convert to action."

**relevance:** validates achiever's requirement to transform vague wishes into articulated goals with `why`, `what`, `how`.

---

### [5] gollwitzer — implementation intentions

**source:** gollwitzer, p. m. (1999). "implementation intentions: strong effects of simple plans." american psychologist, 54(7), 493-503.

**type:** [academic] — meta-analysis effect size d = 0.65

**key quote:** "if-then plans substantially increase the likelihood of goal attainment."

**relevance:** the achiever's `how.task` and `how.gate` create implicit if-then plans: "if I complete task, then I check gate."

---

### [6] elliot & mcgregor — approach vs avoidance

**source:** elliot, a. j., & mcgregor, h. a. (2001). "a 2x2 achievement goal framework." journal of personality and social psychology, 80(3), 501-519.

**type:** [academic]

**key quote:** "approach goals correlate with higher performance and well-being; avoidance goals increase anxiety."

**relevance:** achiever should bias toward approach goals. `what.outcome` describes a positive end state, not what to avoid.

---

### [7] einstein & mcdaniel — prospective memory

**source:** einstein, g. o., & mcdaniel, m. a. (2005). "prospective memory: multiple retrieval processes." current directions in psychological science, 14(6), 286-290.

**type:** [academic]

**key quote:** "prospective memory (memory for intentions) is fragile and benefits from external cues."

**relevance:** validates achiever's persistence to filesystem. `.goals/` provides external cues that enable goal recall across sessions.

---

### [8] dreyfus & dreyfus — skill acquisition

**source:** dreyfus, h. l., & dreyfus, s. e. (1986). "mind over machine." free press.

**type:** [book]

**key quote:** "experts often operate on tacit knowledge that resists articulation."

**relevance:** warns that explicit articulation may slow experts. achiever balances thoroughness with speed — terse articulation for simple tasks.

## industry standards

### [9] doran — SMART goals

**source:** doran, g. t. (1981). "there's a S.M.A.R.T. way to write management's goals and objectives." management review, 70(11), 35-36.

**type:** [practitioner] — foundational PM framework

**key quote:** "goals should be specific, measurable, assignable, realistic, and time-bound."

**relevance:** establishes measurability as core goal property. achiever's `how.gate` provides the measurable criterion.

---

### [10] grove / doerr — OKRs

**source:** grove, a. (1983). "high output management." vintage books. doerr, j. (1999). OKRs at Google.

**type:** [book] / [practitioner]

**key quote:** "objectives are qualitative descriptions of what you want to achieve. key results are quantitative measures."

**relevance:** OKR separates objective (what) from key result (measure). achiever separates `what.outcome` from `how.gate` similarly.

---

### [11] david allen — GTD

**source:** allen, d. (2001). "GTD: the art of stress-free productivity." penguin.

**type:** [book]

**key quote:** "a next action should be the next physical, visible action to move the project closer to its goal."

**relevance:** GTD's inbox → clarify → organize flow matches achiever's asks.inventory → triage → goals flow.

---

### [12] scrum alliance — acceptance criteria

**source:** scrum alliance. "need to know: acceptance criteria." resources.scrumalliance.org.

**type:** [official]

**key quote:** "acceptance criteria are conditions that must be satisfied for a product to be accepted. pass/fail — never partially fulfilled."

**relevance:** validates achiever's binary gate semantic. a goal is fulfilled or not — no partial credit.

---

### [13] atlassian — definition of done

**source:** atlassian. "definition of done." atlassian.com/agile/project-management/definition-of-done.

**type:** [official]

**key quote:** "DoD is a set of high-level criteria to determine if a product increment is complete."

**relevance:** DoD applies to all items; achiever's gate is per-goal. both enforce completion standards.

## cognitive science sources

### [18] control theory — TOTE model

**source:** carver, c. s., & scheier, m. f. (1998). "on the self-regulation of behavior." cambridge university press. (see also PMC article pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6707771/)

**type:** [academic]

**key quote:** "test-operate-test-exit: an organism tests the world against its desired state; operates if there's a discrepancy; repeats until match."

**relevance:** TOTE is the fundamental feedback loop. achiever's triage flow matches: test (uncovered asks?) → operate (create goal) → test → exit.

---

### [19] goal hierarchy

**source:** wiley. "goal hierarchies." onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/joms.13202.

**type:** [academic]

**key quote:** "sub-goals must be instrumental for the primary goal: fulfilment of the sub-goal facilitates fulfilment of the primary goal."

**relevance:** validates achiever's `when.goal` dependency. blocked goals await instrumental goals.

---

### [20] popper — falsifiability

**source:** popper, k. (1959). "the logic of scientific discovery." hutchinson.

**type:** [book] — philosophy of science

**key quote:** "for a statement to be scientific, it must be falsifiable."

**relevance:** achiever's gates must be falsifiable — you can verify pass or fail. vague gates ("make it better") are not falsifiable.

## convergence signals

### goals require specificity

**sources [1, 2, 9] agree:** specific goals outperform vague goals. effect size d = 0.42-0.80.

### goals and tasks are distinct

**sources [3, 11] agree:** goals are desired states; tasks are operators/actions. conflation causes confusion.

### persistence enables recall

**sources [7, 16, 17] agree:** external persistence (filesystem, memory store) enables goal recall across context loss.

### verification must be binary

**sources [12, 13, 20] agree:** acceptance is pass/fail, not partial. gates must be falsifiable.

---

## anti-patterns from sources

### anti-pattern 1: vague goals

**sources [1, 9, 20] warn:** "do your best" goals fail. vague goals are not falsifiable.

### anti-pattern 2: stateless agents

**sources [14, 15] warn:** early agents lost context between loops. achiever persists to filesystem.

### anti-pattern 3: over-articulation

**sources [8] warns:** experts may slow from forced articulation. achiever allows terse articulation for simple tasks.
