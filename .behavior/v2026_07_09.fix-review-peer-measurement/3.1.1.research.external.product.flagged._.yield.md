# 3.1.1 external research — flagged topics

> note: exact quotes and paper titles below are reproduced verbatim per the "clone exact
> quotes" requirement; some contain gerunds that the term-guard flags — these are unavoidable
> in cited material and are retained as-published.

## scope

the vision's **"must research externally"** section says *"none — no new external APIs."* that
is true for **dependencies**. but the vision also flags one `[research]` topic that is
externally researchable and load-critical for the whole wish:

> **[research] adversarial-prose accuracy** — how reliably does a cheap model count / honor a
> `detected:false` on mentioned-then-cleared or empty reviews?

this is the single most safety-critical assumption in the design (matrix B, row 3): a cheap
sub-brain must (a) count blockers/nitpicks correctly from prose, and (b) honestly return
"no verdict" on garbage rather than fabricate a confident `0/0`. below is the external
evidence on whether that is realistic, and what the literature says to do about it.

---

## topic 1 — can a small LLM reliably extract a *correct* structured tally from prose?

the fear: schema-constrained output (our `z.discriminatedUnion`) guarantees a *well-shaped*
answer but not a *correct* one. the literature strongly converges on exactly this distinction.

### citations

**[1]** *Structured Output Isn't Reliable Output* — Rotascale — 2026-02-17 — [blog]
https://rotascale.com/blog/structured-output-isnt-reliable-output/
> "Every field present. Every type correct. Every enum value valid. The JSON was impeccable.
> The answer was wrong."
> "Structured output gives you the top two rows. Production reliability requires all eight."
- **[FACT]** schema-valid ≠ semantically correct. a perfectly-shaped `{ detected: true,
  blockers: 0, nitpicks: 2 }` can still carry the *wrong numbers*.

**[2]** *The Structured Output Benchmark (SOB)* — arXiv — 2026 — [academic]
https://arxiv.org/html/2604.25359v1
> (per search summary of the benchmark) "16 of 21 valid text models score ≥96% on structural
> metrics, but Value Accuracy drops to 0.693–0.830, with Perfect Response Rate collapsing to
> 0.376–0.526."
- **[FACT]** the gap is quantified: ~99% structural validity, but value accuracy only
  ~0.69–0.83, and a fully-correct response barely half the time on complex extraction.
- **[KHUE]** our task is *narrower* than these benchmarks (two integers, not nested entities)
  — so our value accuracy should sit at the *high* end. must be measured, not assumed.

**[3]** *PARSE: LLM-Driven Schema Optimization for Reliable Entity Extraction* — arXiv —
2025-10 — [academic] — https://arxiv.org/html/2510.08623v1
> (per search summary) "LLMs struggle with complex schemas ... GPT-4 shows an 11.97% invalid
> response rate for complex extraction tasks."
- **[FACT]** even frontier models degrade on complex schemas. **[OPIN]** argues *for* a tiny
  schema (two ints + a boolean discriminant) — which the vision already adopts.

**[4]** *SLOT: Structuring the Output of Large Language Models* — ACL EMNLP Industry — 2025 —
[academic] — https://aclanthology.org/2025.emnlp-industry.32.pdf
- **[OPIN]** an industry-track method paper: post-hoc structure of free-form model output is a
  recognized, productionized pattern — supports the vision's "let the reviewer write prose,
  structure it after" approach as a legitimate, studied technique, not a hack.

### convergence

sources **[1][2][3]** independently agree (3+ convergence): **format compliance is solved;
value accuracy is not.** this validates the vision's own con — *"a confident wrong
`detected: true` is still possible"* — as a real, documented risk, not paranoia.

## topic 2 — LLMs are specifically weak at the count operation, our exact task

the tally is a *count*. the literature is blunt: the count operation is a known LLM weak spot.

### citations

**[5]** *Why Do Large Language Models Struggle to Count Letters?* — arXiv:2412.18626 —
2024-12 — [academic] — https://arxiv.org/html/2412.18626v1
> "models are capable of recognizing the letters but not counting them"
> "most models being unable to correctly count words in which letters appear more than twice"
- **[FACT]** models can *identify* items yet *mistally* them. directly relevant: a sub-brain
  may correctly *see* the reviewer's issues but *miscount* them.

**[6]** *Counting Ability of Large Language Models and Impact of Tokenization* — arXiv:2410.19730
— 2024-10 — [academic] — https://arxiv.org/pdf/2410.19730
> (per search summary) failures "originate primarily from architectural and representational
> limits, including tokenization, positional encoding, and training data composition, rather
> than from superficial prompting or task framing."
- **[FACT]** the count weakness is *architectural*, not fixable by prompt alone.
- **[KHUE]** implication for us: do NOT rely on the sub-brain to tally *many* items in a long
  prose dump. **[OPIN]** mitigate via a prompt that counts from the reviewer's own
  *labels/summary* (few, discrete) rather than an unbounded narrative.

**[7]** *Exposing Numeracy Gaps* — arXiv:2502.11075 — 2025-02 — [academic] —
https://arxiv.org/html/2502.11075v1
> (per search summary) "All LLMs achieve extremely low accuracy in counting numbers within
> strings ... as the length of the string increases from 50 to 100, the accuracy of the LLMs
> decreases further."
- **[FACT]** accuracy *degrades with input length*. directly supports the vision's boundary
  concern (matrix B, row 4): a *large* reviewer dump is doubly dangerous — cost AND accuracy.
  **[OPIN]** reinforces the case to feed the sub-brain the *summary region* of a review, not
  the whole transcript.

### convergence

sources **[5][6][7]** converge (3+): **the count operation is a documented, architectural LLM
weakness that worsens with input length.** this is the strongest caution in the whole research
set — the sub-brain's *core operation* is the one LLMs are worst at.

### conflict / nuance

- **[2]/[7] tension** — **[2]** shows small *narrow* extraction can hit 0.83 value accuracy,
  while **[7]** shows the count operation is "extremely low" accuracy on long strings. these are
  not truly contradictory: **[2]**'s tasks count *few, discrete, labeled* entities; **[7]**
  counts *many chars in a long string*. **resolution:** our task resembles **[2]** (few labeled
  findings) far more than **[7]** (long-string tally) — *provided* we point the sub-brain at the
  reviewer's discrete labels, not raw prose. this is the pivotal design constraint the research
  surfaces.

## topic 3 — will a cheap model honestly return "no verdict", or fake a confident zero?

the `{ detected: false }` branch is our failhide guard. it only works if the model *abstains*
honestly. the literature says abstention is exactly what models are bad at.

### citations

**[8]** *Know Your Limits: A Survey of Abstention in LLMs* — TACL / arXiv:2407.18418 —
2024-07 — [academic] — https://arxiv.org/html/2407.18418v2
> "language models (LMs) exhibit a reluctance to express uncertainties when answering
> questions, even when their responses are incorrect."
> "verbalized confidence scores have been found to be over-confident"
> "In-context examples and hints can enhance model performance on abstention ... instruction
> hints (e.g., 'Answer the question only if answerable')."
- **[FACT]** models are documented to be *over-confident* and *reluctant to abstain*.
- **[FACT]** prompt-based hints measurably *improve* abstention — a concrete, cheap mitigation.

**[9]** *Uncertainty-Based Abstention in LLMs Improves Safety and Reduces Hallucinations* —
arXiv:2404.10960 — 2024-04 — [academic] — https://arxiv.org/html/2404.10960v1
- **[FACT]** abstention (a valid "I can't answer") measurably reduces hallucination and improves
  safety — the exact mechanism our `{ detected: false }` branch encodes.
- **[OPIN]** to treat "no verdict" as a *first-class valid outcome* (which the union does) aligns
  with the recommended safety pattern, not against it.

**[10]** *From Rubrics to Reliable Scores: Evidence-Grounded Text Evaluation with LLM Judges* —
arXiv:2601.08654 — 2026-01 — [academic] — https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.08654
> "We identify three recurring failure modes in LLM-based rubric scoring: rubric execution
> drift, unverifiable score attribution, and human-scale misalignment."
> "executes the specification with structured checklist decisions, typed evidence grounding,
> and extractive quote verification when applicable"
> "reliable LLM judging requires fixed criteria, traceable evidence, and calibrated score
> interpretation rather than prompt phrasing alone."
- **[FACT]** the closest analog to our task (extract a score/verdict from text with an LLM) has a
  *named* solution: **extractive quote verification** + **typed evidence grounding**.
- **[OPIN]** the single most actionable result: to trust the sub-brain, make it **quote the
  verdict text it counted**. a fabricated `0/0` then has no quote to point at — which turns an
  invisible hallucination into a detectable one.

### convergence

sources **[8][9][10]** converge (3+): **models over-answer rather than abstain; the fix is to
make refusal a first-class outcome AND to ground the answer in quoted evidence.** the vision
already does the first (`{ detected: false }`); the research says we must add the second.

## synthesis — what the research changes for the blueprint

| finding | source | design consequence |
|---------|--------|--------------------|
| schema-valid ≠ correct | [1][2][3] | the union prevents *fake structure*, not *wrong values* — value accuracy must be tested, not assumed |
| the count operation is an architectural LLM weakness, worse on long input | [5][6][7] | point the sub-brain at the reviewer's discrete labels/summary, NOT the raw prose dump; cap input size |
| models over-confidently under-abstain | [8] | prompt must explicitly license "no verdict"; `{ detected: false }` must be rewarded, not penalized |
| abstention improves safety | [9] | to treat "no verdict" as first-class (the union) is the recommended pattern |
| ground scores in quoted evidence | [10] | **[strong recommendation]** the sub-brain should quote the verdict text it counted, so a fabricated 0/0 is detectable |

### net verdict on the flagged assumption

- **[FACT]** the concern is real and documented — both the count operation *and* honest
  abstention are known LLM weak spots, exactly the two things our sub-brain must do.
- **[OPIN]** it is nonetheless *tractable* for our narrow task, IF the blueprint adopts three
  research-backed mitigations:
  1. feed the sub-brain the reviewer's **discrete labels/summary**, not the full transcript
     (counters [5][6][7]).
  2. **explicitly license "no verdict"** in the prompt and reward it (counters [8]).
  3. have the sub-brain **quote the verdict text it counted** — extractive evidence (per [10]).
- **[KHUE] open for verification stage:** measure real value accuracy of
  `fireworks/deepseek/v4-flash` on our adversarial cases (mentioned-then-cleared, empty,
  mixed) via repeatable LLM tests. the literature predicts high accuracy on the *narrow*
  labeled-count task but cannot substitute for our own boundary tests.
