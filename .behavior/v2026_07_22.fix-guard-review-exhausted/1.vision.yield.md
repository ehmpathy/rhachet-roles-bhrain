# 1.vision — exhausted is not blocked; it unlocks

> wish: `.behavior/v2026_07_22.fix-guard-review-exhausted/0.wish.md`

## the one-line vision

> when a review level goes terminal — **including by exhaustion** — the next level
> unlocks and runs. the driver is told this plainly, and keeps driving. a human is
> pulled in only at the very end, once **every** level is terminal.

---

## the core invariant (non-negotiable)

> **there is NO observable state — no pass, no stdout — where a level reads `exhausted`
> (or otherwise terminal) while a higher level has not yet run.** the instant a level
> turns terminal, the next level runs *in that same pass*. and **no stdout may read as
> "the whole thing is halted" unless EVERY level is terminal.**

this is the bar. a driver must never see l1 `exhausted` alone, nor a "budget exhausted /
halted" message, while l3 still has work to do. if such a state is observable, the wish is
not fulfilled — regardless of whether the control flow beneath is "technically" correct on
the next pass.

two states this forbids outright:

1. **the exhausted-alone pass** — l1 flips to `exhausted`/terminal but l3 shows `awaits` in
   the same output. l3 must run *now*, that same pass.
2. **the false halt** — any "peer reviewer budget exhausted" / "halted, human required"
   text while a higher, non-terminal level still exists.

both must be clamped by explicit boundary tests (see the test-gap list in groundwork), not
merely implied by a happy-path snapshot.

## the guiding principle: escalation to a human is the LAST resort

> the driver exhausts every autonomous path before a human is ever pulled. the human is
> the **final backstop**, not a first stop and not a mid-flow crutch.

this is the spine of the whole wish. the ladder unlocks on exhaustion, the driver keeps
driving, the stdout guides — all so that a human is summoned **only at the very end**, and
even then only when the driver's autonomous options are genuinely spent. the four driver
briefs (D1-D4) each apply this same principle to a different obstacle: a review blocker, a
design fulcrum, a reviewer malfunction. the shared enemy is **premature escalation**.

and because escalation is a last resort, **exhaustion is earned, never casual** (see D1).
the driver can almost always converge (it can converse with reviewers via the contemplation
loop), so **manufactured** exhaustion — budget burned without genuine effort — is a defect.
but some reviewers are genuinely **insatiable**; for those, **earned** exhaustion is
legitimate (that is what the `🌙` marks) — provided the driver leaves a cited record of its
attempts to reason and what would fix the review in the future. the human's attention is the
scarcest resource in the loop; the entire design conserves it.

**this is the keystone that justifies unlock-on-exhaustion.** the reason it is *safe* to let
l3 run the moment l1 exhausts is that we **trust the driver already did everything possible**
to satisfy l1 — genuine convergence attempts, spent to the budget, with a cited record.
exhaustion is not "l1 was skipped"; it is "l1 was worked to the end and could not be
satisfied." given that, l3 SHOULD proceed — no further move at l1 remains for the driver, so
a wait would only stall progress and pull the human early. the earned-exhaustion record (D1)
is what earns that trust; absent it, unlock-on-exhaustion would be a bypass rather than a
warranted advance.

**verified today (evidence)**: the t2 snapshot of
`driver.route.peer-budget-exhaustion-unlocks-level.acceptance.test.ts.snap` renders l1
`exhausted, cached` and l3 `rejected (1/5)` in the **same** stdout — so the mechanism
honors this in that one scenario. but the invariant itself is **not** explicitly clamped
(no negative assertion, no false-halt assertion, several boundary cases absent). closing
that is the load-bearing deliverable.

## deliverables

the wish resolves into six deliverables. D5-D6 clamp + reveal the behavior; D1-D4 teach
the driver the trait behind it.

- **D1 — driver brief: converge with reviewers to terminal.** declares the driver trait:
  work WITH reviewers and converge; when a level exhausts, continue through every reviewer
  you could possibly work with until each is terminal (approved OR exhausted) — only THEN
  is a human pulled. complements the extant `rule.always.converge-with-reviewers` (which
  covers dispute contemplation) with the exhaustion-convergence facet.
  home: `.agent/repo=bhrain/role=driver/briefs/`
  - **exhaustion is the LAST resort — earned through genuine convergence effort, never a
    shortcut.** the healthy outcome of a level is **approval through convergence**. budget is
    meant to be spent on real convergence: every round an honest attempt to (a) address the
    blocker, or (b) articulate — via the contemplation loop — why it holds. a driver that
    burns rounds without genuine engagement to "reach exhaustion" has **manufactured a
    defect** — that is the anti-pattern the brief forbids. exhaustion is the alarm, not a
    casual exit.
  - **BUT some reviewers are genuinely insatiable — and for those, exhaustion is
    legitimate.** the moon glyph (`🌙`) exists precisely because a reviewer can be
    impossible to fully satisfy no matter how well the driver reasons. so exhaustion is not
    *always* the driver's failure. the distinction the brief must draw:
    - **manufactured exhaustion** (rounds burned without genuine effort) = a defect, the
      driver's fault.
    - **earned exhaustion** (genuine convergence attempts, reviewer still insatiable) =
      legitimate, the reviewer's limitation.
    the moon glyph marks the earned case; the driver's job is to make every exhaustion the
    earned kind.
  - **earned exhaustion REQUIRES a cited record — this is the deliverable's teeth.** when a
    driver exhausts a reviewer, it must leave (via the `.taken.by_self` contemplation
    artifacts / a summary the human reads):
    1. **the attempts to reason** — what it tried each round to converge (fixes applied,
       arguments articulated), so the human can see genuine effort was spent, not budget
       burned.
    2. **what would fix the reviews in the future** — a concrete note on how the reviewer
       or its rubric could be improved so this insatiability does not recur (e.g. "rule X is
       ambiguous between A and B", "the rubric demands Y which conflicts with Z"). this
       turns a dead-end into a feed-forward that improves the reviewer.
    an exhausted level with no such record is indistinguishable from a coast — so the record
    is what makes earned exhaustion legible and the reviewer improvable.
  - **convergence is almost always reachable — the driver can converse with reviewers.**
    the contemplation loop (`.given.by_peer` / `.taken.by_self`, threaded back to the
    reviewer via `--as contemplated`) means the driver is never stuck with a bare verdict:
    for every blocker there is a path — either **fix the code**, or **articulate why it
    holds** with concrete evidence and persuade the reviewer to drop it. because that
    conversation exists, most exhaustion is avoidable; the genuinely insatiable reviewer is
    the rare tail, and the cited record above is what proves a given exhaustion sits in that
    tail rather than in the coast. (see the extant `rule.always.converge-with-reviewers`.)

- **D2 — driver howto: navigate fulcrum choices.** a guide for when a big design fulcrum
  surfaces mid-drive:
  - retain each fulcrum in a list; review with the human LAST, never mid-flow
  - prefer the best-guess choice and offer to rework it if the change is clean
  - halt with a blocker ONLY if the rework would not be a clean change — and only after
    every other question is addressed
  - budget can always be added after the rest is approved
  home: `.agent/repo=bhrain/role=driver/briefs/howto.navigate-fulcrum-choices.[guide].md`

- **D3 — driver rule: defer fulcrums to last.** the enforcement twin of D2 —
  `rule.always`: a fulcrum decision is best-guessed + flagged for rework, not halted,
  unless the rework is not a clean change; fulcrum reviews are saved for the very end;
  budget for reworks is added after the rest is approved.
  home: `.agent/repo=bhrain/role=driver/briefs/rule.always.defer-fulcrums-to-last.md`
  - **a hard block is the RARE exception, not a normal exit.** the brief must state the
    ladder of preference plainly: in almost every case there is either (a) a way to **agree
    with the reviewer** (converge), or (b) a way to **clearly decline** with a best-guess +
    a flag to review with the human later. only an **insurmountable, fundamental design
    conflict** — where no best-guess is defensible AND the rework would not be a clean
    change — warrants a `--as blocked`. even then, the block comes **last**, after every
    other question is addressed, and it names the conflict + the options. a driver that
    reaches for a block early or often has broken this rule.

- **D4 — driver rule: diagnose reviewer malfunctions.** when a reviewer returns
  `malfunction` (not a verdict — a broken process, exit code outside the verdict set), the
  driver does NOT blindly escalate. it **diagnoses the cause** and offers a concrete
  resolution, routed to whoever can act:
  - **driver-fixable** (e.g. a bad glob, an absent supply file, a malformed rubric path):
    the driver fixes it and re-arrives.
  - **human-fixable** (e.g. an absent credential, a permission gate, an unfilled keyrack):
    the driver surfaces the exact fix command / step for the human, not a bare "it broke".
  - the escalation, when needed, names the *cause and the fix*, never just the symptom
    (aligns with `rule.require.errors-name-the-fix`).
  home: `.agent/repo=bhrain/role=driver/briefs/rule.always.diagnose-reviewer-malfunctions.md`
  - **note**: `malfunction` is already terminal-for-unlock (isReviewPeerVerdictTerminal),
    so a malfunctioned l1 does not block l3 from a run — but it DOES block passage. this
    rule governs how the driver responds, not the unlock logic (which is unchanged).

- **D5 — stdout narrative** (was R2): at exhaustion, the guard stdout states the level is
  terminal and the next level is now live; reuses extant tree formatters.

- **D6 — boundary tests** (was R3): the 7 clamps in groundwork that prove the core
  invariant — especially the two negative clamps (no exhausted-alone pass, no false halt).

### the spine behind D1-D4: escalation to a human is the LAST resort

all four driver briefs are one principle in four arenas:

> **escalation to a human is the last resort — never the first, never mid-flow.** the
> driver exhausts every autonomous path first, then the human is the final backstop.

| arena | the autonomous path the driver takes FIRST | escalate to human ONLY when |
|-------|--------------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| **D1 review blocker** | converse + converge — fix the code, or articulate why it holds via the contemplation loop | genuine effort stalled against an insatiable reviewer (= earned exhaustion, with a cited record) |
| **D2/D3 design fulcrum** | best-guess the choice + offer rework; address every other question | an insurmountable conflict with no defensible best-guess AND no clean rework — and only last |
| **D4 reviewer malfunction** | diagnose the cause; if driver-fixable, fix it and re-arrive | the fix is genuinely human-only (credential, permission) — with the exact fix named |

the common failure mode across all four is **premature escalation** — a human pulled
before the autonomous path is exhausted. every brief exists to prevent that.

three anti-patterns the briefs forbid, one per brief:
- **D1**: burn budget without genuine engagement → **manufactured** exhaustion is a defect;
  only **earned** exhaustion (genuine effort + cited record vs an insatiable reviewer) is a
  valid exit.
- **D3**: reach for `--as blocked` early or often → a block is the **rare exception**, taken
  last, only for a truly insurmountable conflict.
- **D4**: escalate a malfunction as a bare "it broke" → the driver **diagnoses first** and
  names cause + fix, self-repair when it can.

the human's attention is the scarcest resource in the loop. it is spent only once the
driver has genuinely spent every other path.

## the outcome world

### day-in-the-life (after)

a driver arrives a stone. l1 (linter, budget 2) keeps rejecting. they fix, re-arrive,
fix, re-arrive. on the third arrive, l1 is skipped — its budget is spent. instead of a
dead end, the guard says:

```
🗿 guard: 5.3.verification
   ├─ peer reviews
   │  ├─ r1: linter      (l1, 3/2)  exhausted 🌙   ← terminal (budget spent)
   │  └─ r2: architect   (l3, 1/5)  rejected  🔴   ← unlocked, now running
   │
   └─ the path continues
      ├─ l1 is terminal (exhausted) — it no longer blocks you
      ├─ l3 is now live — address its blockers, then:
      │  └─ rhx route.stone.set --stone 5.3.verification --as arrived
      └─ a human is only needed once every level is terminal
```

the driver never wonders "am I stuck?". the stdout tells them exhaustion is terminal,
that l3 is now the active gate, and exactly what to run next. they keep driving.

## user experience

### usecases & goals

- **primary**: a driver drives a guarded stone to terminal across all peer levels
  without needing a human until the very end.
- **secondary**: a driver understands *why* an exhausted level didn't block them, and
  what the peer-review ladder is doing on their behalf.
- **tertiary (human)**: a foreman is summoned exactly once — at the true end — to
  overrule or approve, not repeatedly mid-ladder.

### contract inputs & outputs they leverage

inputs (unchanged surface — this is the ergonomic win):
- `rhx route.stone.set --stone <s> --as arrived` — the one verb a driver keeps running
- `rhx route.stone.set --stone <s> --as approved` — human-only, at the true end
- `rhx route.guard.budget --for review --add N --peer <slug>` — optional, to grant more
  rounds instead of accepting exhaustion

outputs they read:
- the guard tree, now with an **unlock narrative** when a level is exhausted-terminal
- per-reviewer verdict lines: `approved ✓` / `rejected 🔴` / `exhausted 🌙`
- a "path continues" footer that names the currently-live level and the next command

### what leveraging it looks like (timeline)

```
t0  arrive         → l1 rejects (1/2)          → fix, re-arrive
t1  arrive         → l1 rejects (2/2)          → fix, re-arrive
t2  arrive         → l1 exhausted (3/2, 🌙)    → l3 UNLOCKS + runs → l3 rejects (1/5)
t3  arrive         → l1 still exhausted; l3 rejects (2/5) → fix, re-arrive
t4  arrive         → l3 approved ✓ → ALL terminal → stone passes (or awaits human overrule
                     ONLY if a level ended non-approved-terminal)
```

the driver runs the *same command* the whole way down. the ladder advances itself.

## mental model

### how a driver describes it to a friend

> "each stone has reviewers stacked in tiers. you clear a tier by **converging with the
> reviewer** — you work their feedback until they approve. if you genuinely can't converge
> after honestly spending the tier's rounds, the tier is *done* by exhaustion and the next
> tier opens. either way you keep hitting `--as arrived` and work your way down. the human
> only shows up at the bottom. the point isn't to burn budget to skip tiers — it's to
> actually work each reviewer, so the human is only needed if genuine effort still stalls."

### analogies / metaphors

- **elevator floors** — each level opens only once the one below is settled. approval is
  the clean settle; exhaustion settles it too, but leaves a **defect flag on that floor**
  for the human at the top.
- **a relay** — l1 hands the baton to l3 whether it finished clean (approved) or dropped
  it (exhausted = failed to converge). the race continues either way, but a dropped baton
  is logged as a fault, not celebrated.
- **the smoke alarm** — exhaustion is the alarm, not the exit. it means convergence
  failed here and a human must look. a driver's job is to keep the alarm from ever going
  off, by converging — never to treat its going-off as "done".

### their words vs our words

| driver says | we say |
|-------------|--------|
| "that reviewer gave up / ran out" | `exhausted` (skipped, budget spent) |
| "that reviewer's done with me" | `terminal` verdict |
| "the next reviewer opened up" | level `unlocked` |
| "I'm at the bottom, get a human" | `all levels terminal` → human overrule |
| "it's still on me" | `rejected` (ran, has blockers) — non-terminal |

the durable seam to hold: **terminal ≠ approved**. exhausted is terminal-but-not-approved.
that is the whole crux of the confusion, and the brief + stdout must both make it plain.

## evaluation

### how well it solves the goals

- **"human won't overrule until all levels terminal"** — **already holds** in code: the
  halt gates on `allTerminal` (setStoneAsPassed.ts:386). the work is to *keep* this and
  make its stdout distinguish "true end of ladder" from a mid-ladder pause, so the driver
  reads it correctly.
- **"drivers guided to know exhausted unlocks l3"** — solved by (a) a driver-facing
  brief on the peer ladder, and (b) an stdout unlock-narrative on exhaustion. **this is
  the primary net-new work.**
- **"it actually acts that way"** — the unlock-on-exhaustion behavior **already acts that
  way**; the deliverable is to *clamp it* with comprehensive boundary tests so it cannot
  regress, and to close any small stdout-wording gaps that make correct behavior *read*
  as blocked.

### pros

- one command (`--as arrived`) drives the whole ladder — minimal cognitive load.
- humans interrupted once, at the true end — respects their attention.
- exhaustion becomes legible AND honest — the `🌙` marks earned exhaustion (insatiable
  reviewer, cited record), never a silent bypass.
- no new surface area — the fix is behavior + guidance, not new flags.

### cons / tradeoffs

- **coasting (manufactured exhaustion) is a real hazard the design must actively
  discourage — while still permitting earned exhaustion.** a driver *could* burn budget
  without genuine engagement to reach the bottom — a failure mode, NOT an acceptable path.
  yet a genuinely insatiable reviewer makes *earned* exhaustion legitimate. the design must
  discourage the former without forbidding the latter. defenses: (1) D1 requires every round
  be a real convergence attempt; (2) earned exhaustion demands a **cited record** (attempts
  to reason + what would fix the review), which is exactly what distinguishes it from a
  coast; (3) exhaustion is visibly stamped (`🌙`) so neither kind is silent; (4) the record
  is what earns the trust that justifies unlock-on-exhaustion. the norm is **approval through
  convergence**; earned exhaustion is the rare, accountable tail.
- "terminal but not approved" is a genuinely subtle idea; docs must carry it well or the
  confusion just moves down a level.

### edgecases & pit-of-success

| edgecase | pit-of-success contract |
|----------|-------------------------|
| l1 exhausts, l3 pending | l3 unlocks + runs **in the same pass** ("it should have just done it") — no forced re-arrive |
| l1 *already* fully exhausted on entry | first arrive runs l3 immediately; never a wasted "you're exhausted" halt |
| l1 exhausts AND is the highest level | *now* summon the human — all terminal, none approved |
| l1 exhausts, l3 approves | stone passes; no human needed at all |
| l1 approved, l3 exhausts | all terminal, l3 non-approved → human overrule at the end |
| malfunction / constraint at a level | already terminal-for-unlock today; keep that; still blocks passage → human at end |
| driver re-arrives with no artifact change | cached verdicts; ladder state stable; stdout still names the live level |

## what is awkward?

- **"terminal but not approved" is inherently subtle.** the whole wish exists because
  "exhausted" reads as "failed/blocked". even after the fix, this concept has to be
  taught twice — once in the brief, once in the stdout — or the confusion relocates.
- **the same-pass auto-run vs re-arrive tension.** the wish wants "it should have just
  done it" (auto-run l3 the moment l1 exhausts) *and* "if they mark --arrived again". the
  cleanest pit-of-success is auto-run-in-same-pass, with re-arrive as the idempotent
  fallback. supporting both cleanly (without double-running or racing) is the delicate
  part of execution.
- **the two-faced nature of exhaustion is the subtlest thing to teach.** exhaustion is
  **earned** when a genuinely insatiable reviewer could not be satisfied despite real
  convergence effort (legitimate — the `🌙` marks exactly this), but **manufactured** when a
  driver burns budget without genuine engagement (a defect). the briefs (D1) and stdout (D5)
  must hold BOTH faces without collapse: never romanticize exhaustion into a casual exit,
  never condemn every exhaustion as failure. the discriminator is the **cited record** — the
  driver's attempts to reason + what would fix the review in the future. an exhaustion with
  a real record is earned and legible; one without is indistinguishable from a coast. getting
  this dual framing right — legitimate-but-costly, permitted-but-accountable — is the hardest
  authored nuance in the whole wish.
- **the halt already lives in the right place — so the "fix" is mostly words.** the
  single human-touch point is *already* "all levels terminal" (not "any level exhausts").
  that means the headline deliverable is guidance (a brief) and stdout wording, plus
  boundary tests to clamp behavior that already works. a wish that *sounds* like a
  behavioral bug is largely a **legibility** bug — which is easy to under-scope. the risk
  is building a rewrite where a brief + a stdout footer + tests would do.
